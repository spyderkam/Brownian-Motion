Investigating a Chain of Interacting Beads
¶

$ \hspace{1in}\displaystyle \dot{\boldsymbol{\mathrm r}} = \dfrac{\sum \boldsymbol{\mathrm F}}{b} $

where the sum of the forces is:

$ \hspace{1in}\displaystyle \sum\boldsymbol{\mathrm F} = \boldsymbol{\mathrm F} _{\mathrm{random}} + \boldsymbol{\mathrm F}_{\mathrm{FENE}} + \boldsymbol{\mathrm F}_{\mathrm{excluded \, volume}} $

where $k_{\mathrm{excluded \, volume}} = 0 \,\rightarrow\, \boldsymbol{\mathrm F}_{\mathrm{excluded \, volume}} = 0$.

$ \hspace{1in}\displaystyle \boldsymbol{\mathrm F}_{\mathrm{FENE}}\left(\boldsymbol{\mathrm r}\right) = \frac{3k_{\mathrm{B}}T}{l_{\mathrm{k}}} \left[ \frac{\boldsymbol{\mathrm r}/L_{\mathrm{s}}}{1-{\left(\boldsymbol{\mathrm r}/L_{\mathrm{s}}\right)^2}} \right] $

where $l_{\mathrm{k}}$ is the Kuhn length and $L_{\mathrm{s}}$ is the maximum spring length. The thermal energy is set to unity.

Note that after $1$ relaxation time the radius of gyration drops to $R_{\mathrm{g}}(t = \tau) \approx {A_{\mathrm{g}}}/{e}$. The exact expression is actually $A_\mathrm{g}/e + B_{\mathrm{g}}$ but since $B$ seems to always be small it can be ignored. Same idea applies to the end-to-end radius, $R_{\mathrm{ee}}(\tau) \approx A_{\mathrm{ee}}/e$.
